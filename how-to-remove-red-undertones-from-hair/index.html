How To Remove Red Undertones From Hair Tips And Tricks

Written by Oliver Jul 12, 2023 Â· 7 min read

Are you tired of dealing with red undertones in your hair? Whether you've recently dyed your hair or you're a natural redhead, sometimes those warm tones can be a bit overpowering. Fortunately, there are several ways to neutralize those red undertones and achieve a more balanced hair color. In this article, we'll go over some tips and tricks for removing red undertones from hair.

Table of Contents

  • How to Remove Red Undertones from Hair
  • Step-by-Step Guide
  • Tips and Tricks
  • FAQ
  • Hair Care for Red Undertones

How to Remove Red Undertones from Hair

Before we get started, it's important to note that the process for removing red undertones from hair will depend on your hair type, hair color, and the severity of the red tones. With that being said, here are a few methods to consider:

1. Use a Color-Correcting Shampoo

Color-correcting shampoos are designed to neutralize unwanted tones in the hair. Look for a shampoo that is specifically designed for red undertones or one that contains green or blue pigments, as these colors are opposite to red on the color wheel and can cancel out the warmth in your hair. Use the shampoo once or twice a week to gradually reduce redness.

2. Try a Toner

If your hair is already lightened or bleached, a toner can help to remove red undertones. Toners are semi-permanent hair colors that can be used to neutralize unwanted tones. Look for a toner that is labeled as "ashy" or "cool" and apply it to your hair after shampooing. Leave the toner on for the recommended amount of time and then rinse thoroughly.

3. Use a Hair Mask

A hair mask can be a great way to add moisture to your hair while also neutralizing red undertones. Look for a hair mask that contains ingredients like purple shampoo, apple cider vinegar, or honey, as these can all help to balance out the redness in your hair. Apply the mask to your hair once a week and leave it on for 10-15 minutes before rinsing.

Step-by-Step Guide

Here is a step-by-step guide for removing red undertones from hair:

  1. Choose a method for removing red undertones (shampoo, toner, or hair mask).
  2. Follow the instructions on the product packaging.
  3. Repeat the process as needed until you achieve your desired hair color.

Tips and Tricks

Here are a few additional tips and tricks to keep in mind:

  • Avoid using hot tools on your hair, as heat can cause red tones to become more prominent.
  • Consider using a purple or blue shampoo in between washes to maintain your hair color.
  • Consult with a professional stylist if you're not sure which method to use or if you're concerned about damaging your hair.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about removing red undertones from hair:

Can I use lemon juice to remove red undertones from my hair?

Lemon juice can help to lighten hair, but it's not a reliable method for removing red undertones. In fact, lemon juice can sometimes make red tones more prominent. Stick to using a color-correcting shampoo or toner instead.

Will removing red undertones damage my hair?

It depends on the method you use and the condition of your hair. If you use a harsh chemical treatment or over-process your hair, you could cause damage. Stick to using gentle products and follow the instructions carefully.

Hair Care for Red Undertones

Once you've removed red undertones from your hair, it's important to take care of your hair to maintain your new hair color. Here are a few tips:

  • Use a sulfate-free shampoo and conditioner to avoid stripping your hair of color.
  • Avoid using hot tools on your hair, as heat can cause damage and make red tones more prominent.
  • Consider using a hair mask once a week to keep your hair moisturized and healthy.

By following these tips and tricks, you can successfully remove red undertones from your hair and achieve the hair color you've always wanted. Remember to be patient and gentle with your hair, and consult with a professional stylist if you have any concerns.
